\begin{abstract}

We reformulate the definition of a fibred $\mathcal{K}$-ary lax monoidal monad by utilizing the double-categorical machinery of equipments. By isolating the ``monomial fragment'' of the ambient category, we demonstrate that all coherence axioms—including the notoriously unwieldy base-change compatibility—collapse into the single, elegant condition of being a monoid in the strict monoidal category of lax endo-transformations of the identity.

\end{abstract}


\section{Background: Equipments, Companions, and Conjoints}


To absorb the base-change compatibility into the geometry of the ambient category, we work within a double category. A double category $\mathbb{D}$ consists of objects, tight (vertical) arrows, loose (horizontal) arrows, and square cells. 


\begin{definition}[Companions and Conjoints]

Let $f: A \to B$ be a tight arrow in a double category $\mathbb{D}$. 

\begin{enumerate}

    \item A loose arrow $f_*: A \nrightarrow B$ is a \emph{companion} of $f$ if there exist binding cells exhibiting a double-categorical adjunction where $f_*$ acts as a left adjoint to $f$ across the tight/loose divide.

    \item A loose arrow $f^*: B \nrightarrow A$ is a \emph{conjoint} of $f$ if there exist binding cells exhibiting $f^*$ as a right adjoint to $f$.

\end{enumerate}

\end{definition}


An \emph{equipment} (or fibrant double category) is a double category in which every tight arrow possesses both a companion and a conjoint. For our purposes, we will construct a partial equipment where conjoints exist exactly for our chosen arities.


\section{The Arity Equipment $\mathbb{W}_{\mathcal{K}}(\mathcal{C})$}


Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a category with pullbacks, and let $\mathcal{K}$ be a specified class of ``arity'' morphisms containing all identities and closed under composition. We assume that for every $u: I \to J$ in $\mathcal{K}$, the base-change functor $u^* : \mathcal{C}/J \to \mathcal{C}/I$ has a right adjoint $\Pi_u$ (dependent product), and that the Beck-Chevalley condition holds for pullbacks involving $\mathcal{K}$.


We define a double category $\mathbb{W}_{\mathcal{K}}(\mathcal{C})$ realized internally to $\mathbb{C}\mathrm{at}$ (often viewed as the monomial fragment of the full polynomial equipment):


\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Objects:} The slice categories $\mathcal{C}/I$ for each $I \in \mathcal{C}$.

    \item \textbf{Tight Arrows:} Base change functors. For $w: I \to J$ in $\mathcal{C}$, the tight arrow is $w^* : \mathcal{C}/J \to \mathcal{C}/I$. (The vertical category is $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$).

    \item \textbf{Loose Arrows:} Dependent products. For an arity $u: I \to J$ in $\mathcal{K}$, the loose arrow $I \nrightarrow J$ is $\Pi_u : \mathcal{C}/I \to \mathcal{C}/J$.

    \item \textbf{Squares:} Natural transformations. For a pullback square in $\mathcal{C}$ exhibiting $u'$ as the pullback of $u \in \mathcal{K}$ along $w_1$, the square cell is the canonical Beck-Chevalley comparison $w_1^* \Pi_u \Rightarrow \Pi_{u'} w_2^*$.

\end{itemize}


\begin{lemma}

The double category $\mathbb{W}_{\mathcal{K}}(\mathcal{C})$ is a partial equipment. The fibrancy in the $\mathcal{K}$-direction is precisely witnessed by the adjunctions $u^* \dashv \Pi_u$. Specifically, for any $u \in \mathcal{K}$, the loose arrow $\Pi_u$ is the conjoint of the tight arrow $u^*$, and the Beck-Chevalley condition guarantees that the structural square cells over pullbacks are invertible.

\end{lemma}


\section{Monads as Monoids in $\mathbf{Lax}$}


A monad $T$ acting inside each fibre $T_I : \mathcal{C}/I \to \mathcal{C}/I$ does not move objects in the double category; rather, it acts as a lax endo-transformation of the identity.


\begin{definition}

Let $\mathbf{Lax}(\mathrm{Id}_{\mathbb{W}}, \mathrm{Id}_{\mathbb{W}})$ be the strict monoidal category of lax double natural transformations from the identity double functor on $\mathbb{W}_{\mathcal{K}}(\mathcal{C})$ to itself. The monoidal product is strict composition of transformations.

\end{definition}


A lax transformation $\sigma \in \mathbf{Lax}(\mathrm{Id}_{\mathbb{W}}, \mathrm{Id}_{\mathbb{W}})$ consists of:

\begin{enumerate}

    \item For each object $\mathcal{C}/I$, a tight endo-arrow $T_I : \mathcal{C}/I \to \mathcal{C}/I$.

    \item For each loose arrow $\Pi_u$, a structural square cell $\phi_u : \Pi_u \circ T_I \Rightarrow T_J \circ \Pi_u$.

\end{enumerate}

This data must satisfy loose functoriality (compatibility with loose composition and identities) and tight naturality (compatibility with the squares of $\mathbb{W}_{\mathcal{K}}(\mathcal{C})$).


\subsection{The Mate Lemma}


The primary advantage of this equipment is that Axiom 3 (Base Change Compatibility) ceases to be independent data. 


\begin{lemma}[Mate Correspondence] \label{lem:mates}

Let $u: I \to J$ be an arity in $\mathcal{K}$. Because $\Pi_u$ is the conjoint of $u^*$, we have an adjunction $u^* \dashv \Pi_u$. This adjunction induces a canonical mate bijection between natural transformations:

\[

\mathrm{Nat}(\Pi_u T_I, T_J \Pi_u) \xrightarrow{\cong} \mathrm{Nat}(T_I u^*, u^* T_J)

\]

\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}

Given $\phi_u : \Pi_u T_I \Rightarrow T_J \Pi_u$, we define its conjugate $\bar{\phi}_u : T_I u^* \Rightarrow u^* T_J$ via the composite:

\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=large, row sep=large]

\mathcal{C}/J \arrow[r, "T_J"] \arrow[d, "u^*"'] & \mathcal{C}/J \arrow[d, "u^*"] \\

\mathcal{C}/I \arrow[r, "T_I"'] & \mathcal{C}/I

\arrow[Rightarrow, "\bar\phi_u"{description}, from=1-2, to=2-1, shorten <=8pt, shorten >=8pt]

\end{tikzcd}

using the unit $\eta: \mathrm{Id} \Rightarrow \Pi_u u^*$ and counit $\epsilon: u^* \Pi_u \Rightarrow \mathrm{Id}$. The tight naturality of $\phi$ over the Beck-Chevalley squares in $\mathbb{W}_{\mathcal{K}}(\mathcal{C})$ forces $\bar{\phi}_u$ to be invertible, making the lax transformation ``fibred''.

\end{proof}


\section{The Main Theorem}


We can now cleanly restate the definition of a fibred $\mathcal{K}$-monoidal monad without referencing the unwieldy commutative diagrams of the standard definition.


\begin{theorem}

A fibred $\mathcal{K}$-ary lax monoidal monad on $\mathcal{C}$ is exactly a monoid in the strict monoidal category $\mathbf{Lax}(\mathrm{Id}_{\mathbb{W}}, \mathrm{Id}_{\mathbb{W}})$.

\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}

Let $(T_\bullet, \phi)$ be the underlying transformation of the monoid, with monoid unit $\eta: \mathrm{Id} \Rightarrow T$ and multiplication $\mu: T \circ T \Rightarrow T$. The standard axioms translate directly to the internal double-categorical wiring of the monoid:

\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Axioms 1 \& 2 (Arity Unitality and Associativity):} Corresponds precisely to the lax-functoriality of the transformation $T$ with respect to loose composition ($\Pi_v \circ \Pi_u \cong \Pi_{vu}$) and loose identities ($\Pi_{\mathrm{id}} = \mathrm{Id}$).

    \item \textbf{Axiom 3 (Base Change / Arity Re-indexing):} Corresponds to the tight-naturality of the $\phi$ cells over the base-change squares of $\mathbb{W}_{\mathcal{K}}(\mathcal{C})$. By Lemma \ref{lem:mates}, the compatibility of $\phi_u$ over conjoints is conjugate to the compatibility of $\bar{\phi}_u$ over base-changes.

    \item \textbf{Axioms 4 \& 5 (Unit and Multiplication Compatibility):} Corresponds to the requirement that $\eta$ and $\mu$ are \emph{modifications} (maps of lax transformations), meaning they commute properly with the structural cells $\phi_u$.

\end{itemize}

Thus, the single condition of being a monoid object in $\mathbf{Lax}(\mathrm{Id}_{\mathbb{W}}, \mathrm{Id}_{\mathbb{W}})$ exactly captures the data and coherence of the monad.

\end{proof}


\begin{remark}

The lax/oplax asymmetry of monoidal monads perfectly mirrors the conjoint/companion asymmetry in the equipment. The genuine data $\phi$ lives over the conjoints $\Pi_u$ (which only exist for $u \in \mathcal{K}$). Dually, oplax structures live freely over the companions $\Sigma_u$, which exist for all maps. In short: the oplax direction is free, the lax direction is data.

\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Beck-Chevalley Condition]
Consider a pullback square in the ambient category $\mathcal{C}$:
\begin{tikzcd}[column sep=large, row sep=large]
I' \arrow[r, "u'"] \arrow[d, "w_1"'] & J' \arrow[d, "w_2"] \\
I \arrow[r, "u"'] & J
\end{tikzcd}
where the bottom edge $u$ (and therefore $u'$) is an arity in $\mathcal{K}$. The adjunctions $u'^* \dashv \Pi_{u'}$ and $u^* \dashv \Pi_u$ induce a canonical mate natural transformation across the square, defined as the composite:
\[
\mathfrak{bc} : w_2^* \Pi_u \xrightarrow{\eta'} \Pi_{u'} u'^* w_2^* \cong \Pi_{u'} w_1^* u^* \xrightarrow{\epsilon} \Pi_{u'} w_1^*
\]
The \emph{Beck-Chevalley condition} is satisfied if, for all such pullback squares, this canonical comparison transformation $\mathfrak{bc}$ is a natural isomorphism.
\end{definition}

If K-ary products exist for some infinite set K, is there necessarily a structure map phi_K,X?
The Short Answer

No. If K is an infinite set, a monoidal monad on a Cartesian category does not necessarily have an infinite structure map ϕK,X​:∏k∈K​TXk​→T(∏k∈K​Xk​).
Why Finite Arities Work (and Infinite Ones Don't)

A monoidal monad T on a Cartesian category is equipped with two foundational structure maps:

    A binary map: ϕX,Y​:TX×TY→T(X×Y)

    A nullary map: ϕ∅​:1→T(1)

For any finite set J, the map ϕJ​ is constructed by iteratively composing the binary map ϕX,Y​. Because this construction relies on standard mathematical induction, it is strictly limited to finite arities.

To extend this to an infinite set K, the monad T would need to possess additional limit-preserving properties (specifically, it would need to interact continuously with infinite products). The standard axioms of a monoidal monad simply do not require or guarantee this behavior.
A Concrete Counterexample

We can clearly see why this fails by looking at Set (a Cartesian category that possesses all infinite products).

Consider the List monad T, where T(X) is the set of all finite lists of elements from X. This is a well-known monoidal monad where the binary map ϕ takes two finite lists and returns a finite list of pairs.

Let K=N (the infinite set of natural numbers).

    The Domain ∏k∈N​T(Xk​): An element here is an infinite sequence of finite lists.

    The Codomain T(∏k∈N​Xk​): An element here is a single finite list whose elements are infinite sequences.

If you attempt to construct ϕN​, the structural mismatch becomes obvious. Suppose you have an infinite sequence of lists, and each list contains exactly 2 elements. To "zip" or sequence these together preserving all combinations, you would need to produce an uncountably infinite number of infinite sequences. However, the codomain dictates that your output must be a single finite list.

There is no structural, natural way to map an infinite sequence of finite lists into a finite list of infinite sequences without violating the naturality or unit coherence conditions required of a monoidal structure map. Therefore, ϕK​ cannot exist in the general case.
